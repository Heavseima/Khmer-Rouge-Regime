Introduction

Welcome to an exploration of Cambodia under the Khmer Rouge, one of the most tragic and devastating periods in modern history. The Khmer Rouge regime, which ruled Cambodia from 1975 to 1979, was led by Pol Pot, who sought to transform the country into a rural, self-sufficient society. In doing so, the regime purged the country of perceived enemies and imposed radical social and economic changes that resulted in the deaths of millions.

This genocide highlights the catastrophic consequences of ideologically-driven regimes and their ability to exploit power, leaving a profound impact on the lives of those who suffered and shaping the future of Cambodia for generations to come. The following sections of this project will explore the factors that led to the Khmer Rouge's rise, the policies they implemented, and the lasting legacy of their reign.

Three Major Key Roles

War: The Rise of Khmer Rouge

The Khmer Rouge capitalized on regional conflicts and internal instability to gain power in Cambodia. Their rise was deeply tied to the consequences of war and foreign intervention.

  • The Vietnam War Spillover: As the Vietnam War escalated, Cambodia’s neutrality was violated. The U.S. and South Vietnamese forces frequently crossed into Cambodian territory to target Viet Cong supply routes, destabilizing the country.
  • U.S. Bombing Campaign: Between 1969 and 1973, the U.S. carried out massive bombing raids in Cambodia, targeting suspected Viet Cong hideouts. These bombings devastated rural areas, killed tens of thousands, and displaced millions, fueling resentment against the government.
  • Civil War: The chaos created by U.S. intervention and the increasing involvement of Vietnamese communists led to a full-blown civil war between the Cambodian government, led by Lon Nol, and the Khmer Rouge.
  • Vietnamese Influence: The North Vietnamese, supporting the Khmer Rouge as part of their broader communist strategy, provided resources and training, strengthening the insurgents.

These factors weakened Cambodia’s government, destabilized its economy, and turned rural populations against the regime, allowing the Khmer Rouge to present themselves as liberators and gain significant support. By 1975, they seized Phnom Penh and took control of the country.

State Power Under the Khmer Rouge

The Khmer Rouge established an authoritarian state where power was centralized entirely within the Communist Party of Kampuchea (CPK). This control allowed the regime to enforce its radical vision with absolute authority.

  • Centralized Leadership: State power was concentrated in the hands of Pol Pot and a small cadre of party leaders. They made all key decisions, bypassing any traditional forms of governance.
  • Abolishment of Institutions: The Khmer Rouge dismantled all existing state institutions, including the monarchy, judiciary, and local administration, to eliminate opposition and consolidate control.
  • Enforcement Through Violence: State power was maintained through fear, with mass executions, forced labor camps, and widespread surveillance used to silence dissent and eliminate perceived enemies.
  • Control Over Daily Life: Every aspect of life, from work to food distribution, was managed by the state. Individuals had no personal freedoms, as the regime controlled housing, movement, and even relationships.

This consolidation of state power enabled the Khmer Rouge to impose its radical policies but resulted in widespread oppression, fear, and devastation across Cambodia.

Khmer Rouge Class System

The Khmer Rouge implemented a strict class hierarchy to enforce their vision of a revolutionary society.

  • The New People: Urban citizens, intellectuals, and professionals forcibly relocated to rural areas. They were viewed as "enemies" of the revolution and often subjected to brutal labor, starvation, and execution.
  • The Old People: Rural farmers who were seen as more aligned with the Khmer Rouge’s ideals. They were forced into collective farming but generally faced less severe treatment compared to the New People.
  • The Base People: Khmer Rouge loyalists from rural areas who were given privileges and trusted with enforcing the regime’s policies.

This system created deep divisions within society, favoring rural revolutionaries and punishing those from urban or educated backgrounds. It was a tool of control that led to immense suffering and the systematic destruction of Cambodian culture and life.

Three Key Individuals

Pol Pot

Pol Pot

Pol Pot, born Saloth Sâr, was the leader of the Khmer Rouge and the architect behind the radical policies that defined Democratic Kampuchea from 1975 to 1979. He sought to create a classless, agrarian society, but his vision led to one of the most brutal genocides in modern history. Pol Pot's leadership is marked by extreme violence, widespread suffering, and the loss of nearly two million lives.

  • Educated in France, where he was influenced by Marxist ideas
  • Led the Khmer Rouge during the Cambodian genocide
  • Enforced policies aimed at creating an agrarian, classless society
  • Ordered mass executions and widespread starvation
  • Oversaw the establishment of labor camps and Tuol Sleng prison
Heng Samrin

Heng Samrin

Heng Samrin was initially a Khmer Rouge commander before defecting and playing a key role in overthrowing Pol Pot's regime. In the wake of the regime’s fall, Samrin emerged as a key figure in the new government, helping to stabilize Cambodia after years of terror. His transition from revolutionary to leader marked a significant shift in the country’s path toward recovery.

  • Initially served as a Khmer Rouge commander before defecting in 1978
  • Led a faction of former Khmer Rouge members and aligned with Vietnamese forces to overthrow Pol Pot's regime
  • Became a leader in the People's Republic of Kampuchea after the Khmer Rouge's fall in 1979
  • Played a crucial role in stabilizing Cambodia in the post-genocide period and setting the foundation for its recovery
Chum Mey

Chum Mey

Chum Mey is one of the few survivors of Tuol Sleng (S-21), the infamous prison where thousands of Cambodians were tortured and executed during the Khmer Rouge era. His survival is a testament to the resilience of those who endured the horrors of the regime. Since his release, Chum Mey has dedicated his life to telling his story and advocating for justice and remembrance.

  • Survived Tuol Sleng prison (S-21), where thousands were tortured and executed under the Khmer Rouge regime
  • Has become an advocate for justice and remembrance, sharing his story to preserve the memory of the victims
  • Works to educate future generations on the atrocities of the Khmer Rouge regime
  • Testified in various international courts such as UN's ICJ on case number 001, 002, 003 and forums to ensure that the history of the Khmer Rouge atrocities is not forgotten

Arrival of Salvation Front

Movement of Resistance

In 1978, the United Front for the National Salvation of Kampuchea was established to overthrow the Khmer Rouge and end the suffering caused by their oppressive regime. The movement was led by Heng Samrin, a former Khmer Rouge official who defected after witnessing the regime’s atrocities. Vietnam played a crucial role in supporting the Front, providing military assistance, training, and leadership to bolster their efforts. This marked a pivotal moment in Cambodia’s history, as it symbolized both internal resistance and international intervention.

  • United defectors and Cambodian resistance groups to overthrow the Khmer Rouge.
  • Coordinated with Vietnam to launch a successful military offensive in late 1978.
  • Captured Phnom Penh on January 7, 1979, marking the end of Khmer Rouge rule.
  • January 7 is celebrated as Victory Day, symbolizing Cambodia’s liberation.
  • Initiated efforts to rebuild the nation while facing a legacy of Vietnamese influence.

With the fall of Phnom Penh and the Khmer Rouge ousted, the Salvation Front established the People’s Republic of Kampuchea, backed by Vietnam. While the movement brought an end to a horrific chapter, it also sparked debates about sovereignty and foreign intervention. Nevertheless, the Salvation Front remains a key chapter in Cambodia’s fight for freedom and recovery.

5 Cs of Historical Thinking

Change Over Time

The Khmer Rouge regime which is from 1975 to 1979 marked a profound transformation in Cambodia, moving from a monarchy with colonial influences to an isolated agrarian state under radical rule. Their policies abolished urban life, dismantled educational institutions, and eradicated perceived capitalist elements to forge a "pure" peasant society. The forced displacement, collectivization, and brutal repression left lasting scars, reshaping Cambodian society and impacting the nation's development and trauma that resonate today.

Context

The Khmer Rouge took power during a time of Cold War tensions and unrest from the Vietnam War. The Cambodian government was weak after U.S. bombings, which helped the Khmer Rouge rise to power. Their ideas were a mix of radical communism and nationalism, and they wanted Cambodia to be self-sufficient and isolated from the rest of the world. This desire for self-reliance led them to close off the country and reject foreign aid, which worsened the crisis.

Casuality

Several factors caused the rise of the Khmer Rouge, including political chaos, economic problems, and anger over foreign interference. Their goal was to create a classless society by getting rid of people they saw as enemies, like intellectuals and city-dwellers. But this led to mass deaths, starvation, and suffering. The Khmer Rouge's policies of forced labor and extreme punishment created a widespread humanitarian disaster that left the country deeply scarred.

Contingency

Key events, like the Khmer Rouge's partnership with China and their decision to get rid of money, were influenced by specific leaders and circumstances. For example, U.S. bombing made rural people angry, which helped the Khmer Rouge gain support. The Vietnamese invasion in 1978 also played a key role in ending the Khmer Rouge's rule. This invasion, along with internal problems within the regime, led to their downfall and the end of their extreme policies.

Complexity

The Khmer Rouge affected people in different ways. Rural people were forced to work on farms, while city people and intellectuals faced harsher treatment. International reactions were mixed, with some condemning the regime and others, due to Cold War politics, indirectly supporting them. Within Cambodia, some people secretly resisted the regime, showing how individuals found ways to survive under extreme oppression. These responses highlight the complexity of survival and resistance in such a brutal system.
